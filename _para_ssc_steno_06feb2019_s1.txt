The process of turning water vapour back into liquid water is called condensation. The opposite of vaporisation, condensation is the transition of matter’s physical condition from the gaseous to the liquid state. Condensation begins when atomic or molecular clusters of that species form within the gaseous volume of the substance—much as how raindrops or snowflakes form within clouds—or when the substance’s gaseous phase makes contact with a liquid or solid surface. A ‘condenser’ is a piece of equipment for compressing and chilling vapour that gathers condensed liquids. A sort of vaporisation called evaporation takes place on the surface of a liquid when it transitions into the gas phase. When a liquid’s temperature drops below its freezing point, a phase transition called freezing occurs, converting it from a liquid to a solid. Without going through the intermediary liquid phase, sublimation is the process by which a substance moves straight from the solid to the gas phase.
